Making sense of data

Back to basics

What do we mean by 'making sense of data'? It can't be that complicated. Surely that's what graphs and stuff are for ... just shove your data into some kind of chart and the job is done, right?
As it turns out - no, not really.
Data visualisation is about so much more than visualising data. It's a commmunication, a set of design choices around brand and audience, and most of all it's a response.

A response to what?

Well, it's kind of your job to figure that out.

  • What question does your chart answer?
  • What problem does it solve?
  • Who will look at it and as a result feel ready to take action or make a decision?

To explore this point, here's an example using some data from one of my hobbies.

First, find some data

I spend several hours a week playing Destiny 2, an online MMORPG first-person shooter. Like many games in this genre it has a player-versus-player (PvP) mode where you can test your skills against other players in a controlled arena separate from the main story missions. Tensions run hot in this type of mode, and accusations of cheating are commonplace.
Being a middle-aged gamer with below-average reflexes, I frequently come off worse in these competitions, and of course that's very frustrating. Sometimes I find myself matched against people so skilled, so godly, that I never stood a chance of beating them.
When that happens I find myself hovering over the 'Report' button and asking myself this question ...

'Do I really believe they're cheating, or am I just salty about losing?'.

Reinventing the wheel?

There are many existing tracker sites and apps for Destiny 2, and they're each good at different things. Inventory management apps let you access your gear and resources while playing, to swap items over without having to return to aa specific lcoaation to access your vaault. Others provide performance and ranking information so you can see how you're doing and who you're up against.
These are all good, but the trackers in particular tend to have a lot of blinking or scrolling content - mostly adverts but also banners, buttons and links. It's a very cluttered interface and not one I enjoy looking at or having open oin a second screen while I'm trying to play. Added to that, to get up-to-the-minute data I would need to stay logged in constantly. I need something much more basic than that - a simple visual look up that tells me at a glance whether I'm justified in reporting a player or whether I just need to get over it.

There's a question to be answered!

One thing that sets Destiny as a game apart from many others is its very rich, well-structured and well-documented API, hence the rich ecosystem of supporting apps and services. With a developer account I can access huge amounts of game data for myself and other players, and using that I can do pretty much any analysis that takes my fancy.
This is a very dangerous scenario for any data scientist. All this data, and no clear question ...
I wondered if it would be possible to extract the right data to analyse who the likely cheats are in the games I'd played recently. That sort of thing would show up in their game statistics, right? Simple. All I needed to do was extract all of the game stats and then try some different models until I got an answer.
For the machine learning bit, I ran through various different model and data structures before admitting that, actually, I couldn't identify any meaningful pattern.
This just in - good players tend to be good, more at ten.
And the more I tweaked the models, the more I lost sight of the problem I was originally trying to solve. I also developed immense sympathy for the developers who work on anti-cheat tools!

models are just far too accurate - something here isn't right id'd one player as 'dubious' who is actually just very, very good including class - lots of people who play a lot of pvp favour hunter class. one of the samples (a known cheat, banned recently and very publicly) only plays on hunter

Lie down, try not to cry, cry anyway ...

So if my analysis isn't answering my question, what does? Time to go back and reframe the question.

What do I really want to know?

Firstly: should I have expected this person to beat me?
Signs for that include:

  • Are they using the meta?
  • Are they are using known exploits?
  • Are they using particlar exotic weapons or armour?
  • Are they at a significantly higher level than me?

If one or more of those things is true, perhaps I just need to 'git gud' ...

One way to explore possible answers to these questions is to look at someone's game history to see whether their win status or their kill count seems feasible. Some of the other tracker sites include a player's Elo ranking, which many take as a relevant metric. I prefer not to, however, and here's why. It only counts wins and losses. Win a game, and you move up. Lose a game and you move down. In a team game, where there can be (depending on the specific mode) 4 or 6 players on each side, it's far too blunt an instrument. Someone with a high success rate could simply have been teamed up with very high-skill players - a 'carry', where the lower-ranking player gets an easy ride with their team mates doing all the hard work. By the same token someone with a streak of losses may actually be very good, but has been matched with lower-ranked teammates in an effort to balance the lobby. So Elo, which was designed for ranking one-on-one performance in chess, is not helpful here.
The game developers have their own combat rating which (while I don't know all the details) does take into account each player's individual performance and contribution to the match result. I decided this would be interesting to include in my analysis, and along with that we can also extract very precise and detailed data on the percentage of precision shots, which indicates skill, or the usage of particular weapon archetypes and character builds which suggest careful tuning of attributes and mods.

Working out how to frame it

Looking at some of these stats in a quick look up, one card per player, it could be helpful. Certainly having the per-game average to compare to the match in question seems like a useful indicator of unusual circumstances which might point to cheating. But is this the right way to display it? Are the colours informative, or just distracting? Are the items in a logical or intuitive order for the reader?
Now we're into design territory.
