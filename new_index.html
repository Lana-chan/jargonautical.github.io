Making sense of data

Back to basics

What do we mean by 'making sense of data'? It can't be that complicated. Surely that's what graphs and stuff are for ... just shove your data into some kind of chart and the job is done, right?

Not really.

"People don't want data;
they want answers."

Lucy Knight, Data Scientist

Data visualisation is about so much more than visualising data. It's crafting a message. It's a set of design choices around brand and audience. And most of all it's a response.

A response to what? Well, it's kind of your job to figure that out. Here's what you need to be asking yourself.

  • What question does your chart answer?
  • What problem does it solve?
  • Who will look at it?
  • Will it help them to take action or make a decision?

To explore this point, let's dive into an example.

Defining the problem

I spend several hours a week playing Destiny 2, an online MMORPG first-person shooter. Like many games in this genre it has a player-versus-player (PvP) mode where you can test your skills against other players in a controlled arena separate from the main story missions. Tensions run hot in this type of gameplay, and accusations of cheating are commonplace.

Being a middle-aged gamer with below-average reflexes I often come off worse in these competitions, and of course that's very frustrating. Occasionally I get matched against people so skilled, so godly, that I never stood a chance of beating them.

When that happens I find myself hovering over the 'Report' button and asking myself this question ...

"Do I really believe they're cheating - or am I just upset because I lost?".

Reinventing the wheel?

There are several support apps for Destiny 2, and they're each good at different things. Inventory management apps let you access your gear and resources while playing, to swap items over without having to return to a specific location to access your vault. Others provide performance and ranking information so you can see how you're doing and who you're up against.

These are all good, but the performance trackers have a lot of blinking or scrolling content - mostly adverts but also banners, buttons and links. It's a very cluttered interface and not one I enjoy looking at or having open on a second screen while I'm trying to play.

What I need is a simple visual look up that tells me at a glance whether I'm justified in reporting a player, or whether I just need to get over it.

Getting hold of some data

One thing that sets Destiny as a game apart from many others is its very rich, well-structured and well-documented API, hence the varied ecosystem of supporting apps and services. With a developer account I can access game data for myself and other players, and using that I can do pretty much any analysis that takes my fancy.

I started with an assumption: it should be possible to get some data to analyse who the likely cheats are in the games I'd played recently. That sort of thing would show up in their game statistics, wouldn't it? Simple. All I needed to do is extract all of the game stats and then try out different machine learning models until I get an answer.

And this is exactly what I did, using the API to extract a massive file for a sample of players. I included myself as a control (I know I'm not cheating!). For the same reaason I included a couple of friends who I know absolutely would never do so, and for good measure I got data on a small number of professional streamers with excellent reputations for fair play. For balance I then included at least one known cheater (recently and very publicly banned) and a few people I've played against in the past and had doubts about.

Armed with this data, I built various different models and reviewed the results. I won't bore the reader with the details of each one because, as it turned out, none of them could identify any meaningful pattern.

So what could the data tell me?

Well, skilled players tend to be good across both competitive and casual game modes, and they play more matches overall (getting in a lot of practice). Obviously they win more, with a higher kills-to-deaths ratio (K/D), and more kills per game. They also do better on the percentage of precision shots with difficult-to-master weapons like sniper rifles.

Lots of these are also what you'd expect to see if someone were cheating ... so what's the crucial indicator for high-skill versus illegal mods?

The k-means model shown above looked promising, but when I delved into it further with a principal components analysis it indicated that match duration and total team score (the number of kills by everyone in the player's team combined) were most significant factors in whether that person was likely to be cheating. That clearly can't be right in this context.

My models either made no sense at all, or were impossibly 100% efficient. Frequently both at once! And the more different models I built and the more I tweaked the parameters, the more I lost sight of the problem I was originally trying to solve.

Time for a sense check

Thinking about all the factors I'd added to the model, it was obvious that:

  • there were too many, and
  • many of them were redundant.

A good example of this is the choice of stats about number of kills. Average score per life = how many kills before the player is brought down by an opponent. This will be reflected in the kills-to-deaths ratio and also efficiency. Number of kills overall is strongly correlated to score, and so on.

Another factor which could be skewing the analysis is the player's choice of class. Looking at the balance of classes in my data I could see that frequent PvP players are more likely to play hunter, an archetype known for agility and stealth, and several of my sample group play ONLY on hunter and have no characters in either of the other classes.

So if my analysis isn't answering my question, what will? What is the question, anyway?

Player classes

  • Warlock (healing and support). Can fly, also look fabulous in robes.
  • Hunter (mobile and sneaky). Like to jump a lot, hard to catch.

What do I really want to know?

Here's the question I should have been asking.

Should I have expected this person to beat me?

I don't need machine learning for that. I just need to look at the other player's character, their build and gear choices, and then use my own understanding of the game's context to make a judgement.

  • Are they using the strongest weapon types?
  • Are they are using known exploits?
  • Are they using particlar exotic weapons or armour that add extra abilities?
  • Are they at a significantly higher level than me?

And of course I need to take a good hard look at myself too.

  • Are we playing on a PvP map that I'm not very familiar with?
  • Am I using the right set up and play style for that map?
  • Did I equip any weapons or abilities that I haven't had much practice using?

In the heat of the moment it's not easy to be that self-reflective, but it's a good habit to develop. It's entirely possible that nobody here is cheating, and I just need to 'git gud'.

We're getting there now. We can certainly do a comparison of gear, level and also player history to see whether their win status or their kill count seems feasible.

Existing tracker apps can help here, but they usually centre around a player's Elo rating. I find this less meaningful in context, because it's calculated purely on wins and losses. Win a game and you move up, lose a game and you move down. In a multiplayer activity where there can be (depending on match settings) up to six players per team, it's not necessarily an accurate meaasure of each player's performance.

For example, someone with a high Elo could simply have been teamed up with very high-skill players - a 'carry', where the less-skilled player gets an easy ride with their team mates doing all the hard work. By the same token someone with a streak of losses may actually be very good, but has been repeatedly matched with lower-ranked teammates in an effort to balance the lobby. So Elo, which was designed for ranking one-on-one performance in chess, is not helpful here.

Destiny 2 players are instead assigned a 'combat rating' which takes into account each player's individual performance and contribution to the match result. Along with that we can also access very detailed data on things like the percentage of precision shots, which indicates skill, or the usage of particular weapon archetypes and character builds which suggest careful tuning of attributes.

The chart to the right shows what some of that data looks like when plotted against kill count and all-time per-game average. This is starting to look like something that could help with my particular question.

Working out how to frame it

Here's where I abandoned complex models and wrote a script to pull out the results of my latest match. Each time I play, if I have doubts, I can refresh this page and take a quick look at each player to see if I can spot any unusual patterns.

Certainly having each player's all-time per-game average to compare to the match in question seems like a useful indicator of unusual circumstances which might point to cheating.

It's progress, but there are still some issues. For one, it's ugly! I built on something far more beautiful, this tutorial on connecting to an API with javascript, and played around with the colours to show at a glance the win/loss status (the colour at the top of the card) and the comparative combat rating (the stripe in the centre). I didn't like how it looked, but it's a start. Now I need to make some decisions about the way I'm displaying this data. Is the colour coding informative, or just distracting? What colur scheme might I go with if I decide to keep it? Are the items in a logical or intuitive order for the reader? What do I want to stand out?

Now we're into design territory.

First pass at a card display in Javascript

Designing the thing

I want the comparisons to be obvious at a glance. Is this person doing much, much better than they normally would? Then we have three dimensions to convey.

  1. direction of travel (better/worse)
  2. the extent of the difference (big/small), and
  3. a value judgement (good/bad) perhaps assigned to colours.

We could use a single symbol for all three dimensions. An up-arrow or down-arrow to convey direction, a colour to represent good/bad, and the arrow size relative to the difference between this match and the usual average.

Or we could have a small plot showing the difference, leave out the colours and let the x-axis position or the labelling indicate which is this match and which is the usual average.

Finally we could use text formatting. the colour of the text or its background could represent good/bad, and the font size could be relative to the difference.
