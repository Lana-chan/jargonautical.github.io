Study of a gaming group network

Gaming groups, like any social network, see a general background level of churn amongst their members. This can be due to life changes, shifting priorities, or evolving preferences for different game genres.

Here we're looking at the changes over time in one such group, looking specifically at the amount of time the members spend in activities with each other. It's possible to see the stronger links (players have joined activities together several times over a given period) and the weaker connections where two players may seldom cross paths even though they belong to the same clan.

And like all social groups, sometimes sub-groups form - players drift towards those who have similar perspectives, interests or schedules and more often can be found in activites with this new subset. And while fractures within the group may seem at the time to be a complete surprise, with hindsight they're easy to see developing.

The buttons here let you filter for different periods in the life of the group, seeing how it evolves.

  1. The original small friend group, playing together regularly.
  2. Recruitment of new members.
  3. New members invite their friends to join.
  4. Distinct clusters form led by pre-existing friendships.
  5. The split - a player is kicked from the group for problematic behaviour, and their cluster follows.
  6. Regrouping; still a little interaction between the clusters, but very reduced.
  7. Present day - no interaction at all between the two clusters, and the breakaway cluster is seeing fractures of its own.

This is a common and recurring cycle, and particularly as gaming groups increase in size - like any community, it requries nurturing and management. Could the group have seen this coming? Only by monitoring in-game interactions, as shown in the graph here. Should a group leader be monitoring in this way? I would say, 'no' ... it's an interesting study after the fact, but uncomfortably invasive at any other time. All of this data has been anonymised for that reason - while members of the two groups might recognise the patterns here, there's no need to call people out.

It's only a game, after all!
